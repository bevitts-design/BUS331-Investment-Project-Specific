⚠ The Silo Error — Most Common Mistake in Phase 4

If your Phase 1 forecasts falling inflation and rate cuts, selecting inflation-hedge commodity plays (e.g., oil ETFs, TIPS overweight) contradicts your own thesis. The Investment Committee will catch this immediately. Every security pick must be defensible using your Phase 1 macro narrative.

The Investment Thesis — Required Format

One page per security — this is your written defense before the oral defense

The investment thesis is the written record of why this security belongs in this client's portfolio. It must connect the security's characteristics to: (1) the macro thesis from Phase 1, (2) the client's IPS constraints from Phase 2, and (3) its role within the Phase 3 asset allocation weights. A thesis that does not make all three connections will not score at the Exceptional level.

The Mandatory Derivative Hedge

Every client requires a derivative strategy that floors downside within IPS limits

The IPS drawdown limit is a hard constraint — not a suggestion. If the Phase 3 Bear Case stress test shows the unhedged portfolio could breach that limit, the derivative hedge is the mechanism that closes the gap. Your team must select, price, and justify one specific strategy per client and explain why you chose it over the alternatives.

The Three Main Strategies — Compare Before You Choose

🛡 Protective Put

What it is: Buy put options on a broad index (e.g., SPY) or individual holdings. Gives the right to sell at the strike price if the market falls.

  • PRO: Precise downside floor — you know your worst case
  • PRO: Keeps full upside participation
  • CON: Premium cost erodes returns even if market rises
  • CON: Must be renewed (premiums recur)

Best for: Risk-averse or moderate clients with defined drawdown limits who need insurance without giving up upside.

✓ Selected for Priya Mehta — see worked example below
📞 Covered Call

What it is: Sell call options on holdings you already own. You collect premium income but cap your upside at the strike price.

  • PRO: Generates income (reduces net cost basis)
  • PRO: Provides partial downside cushion via premium collected
  • CON: Caps upside — if market rallies past strike, gains are forfeited
  • CON: Limited downside protection in a severe crash

Best for: Income-focused or income-seeking clients with moderate upside expectations and willingness to cap gains.

Not selected — upside cap conflicts with Priya's 20-yr growth goal
💵 Cash Re-Allocation

What it is: Not a derivative — shift portfolio weight from risky assets to cash/T-bills to reduce portfolio beta and potential drawdown directly.

  • PRO: No premium cost
  • PRO: Simple to execute and explain
  • CON: Directly reduces expected return
  • CON: Does not provide a precise floor — still exposed to remaining equity

Best for: Very conservative clients (high A value) or when option premiums are prohibitively expensive relative to the portfolio size.

Not selected — return drag too high given Priya's 8.5% IPS target
The Q&A Will Ask This

The Investment Committee's Q&A bank includes: "Why did you choose a Protective Put instead of a Covered Call or simply increasing your Cash allocation? Compare the cost-benefit." Your team must be able to answer this from memory — referencing the client's IPS target return, time horizon, and your macro outlook simultaneously.

Protective Put — Mechanics and Pricing

Protective Put — Key Relationships
Effective Floor = Strike Price − Premium Paid
Net Cost of Hedge = Put Premium × Number of Contracts × 100 shares/contract
Break-Even = Current Price + Premium Paid
Max Loss (hedged) = Current Price − Strike Price + Premium
One standard equity options contract covers 100 shares. To hedge a $90,000 SPY position at ~$500/share, you need 180 shares, which rounds to 2 contracts covering 200 shares. Always document the slight over/under-hedge and justify it.

Put prices increase with: higher volatility (VIX), longer time-to-expiration, and deeper in-the-money strikes. In a Bear Case environment, VIX typically spikes — purchasing puts before volatility rises is significantly cheaper.

Protective Put Strategy on SPY

Priya's Bear Case showed a potential −14.2% portfolio loss — exceeding her IPS drawdown limit of −12%. After re-allocating in Phase 3 (reducing equity to 40%), the team added a Protective Put on SPY to floor the remaining equity risk. Here is the full specification.

Parameter Team's Choice Explanation
Underlying SPY (S&P 500 ETF) Hedging the broad U.S. equity sleeve, which represents 40% of the portfolio ($200,000)
Strike Price 5% out-of-the-money Allows a 5% decline before protection kicks in — absorbs normal volatility without triggering the hedge, keeping premium cost lower
Expiration 6-month (180-day) Covers the primary Bear Case window identified in Phase 1; aligns with the team's macro forecast horizon for Fed action
Number of Contracts 4 contracts (covers ~$200,000) SPY ≈ $500; 4 contracts × 100 = 400 shares × $500 = $200,000. Slight over-hedge is acceptable and documented.
Estimated Premium ~$18 per share ($7,200 total) Based on VIX ~18 and 5% OTM 6-month put pricing. Represents 1.44% of the hedged position — the "insurance premium"
Effective Floor −9.8% Bear Case loss (hedged) Down from −14.2% unhedged. Safely within the −12% IPS drawdown limit. ✓ PASS.
✓ IPS Compliance Confirmed

The hedged Bear Case portfolio loss of −9.8% is within Priya's IPS tripwire of −12%. The hedge cost of $7,200 (1.44% of the equity sleeve) is justified by the fiduciary obligation to protect the client's capital within the mandate. This trade-off — sacrificing 1.44% of return potential for IPS compliance — must be explained explicitly in the Phase 6 oral presentation.

Documenting the Hedge in Your Submission

W
Written Submission Requirements

  • Strategy name and underlying security
  • Strike, expiration, number of contracts
  • Premium paid and source of pricing data
  • Effective floor calculation (show the math)
  • Comparison to at least one alternative strategy
  • IPS compliance confirmation: hedged Bear Case ≤ drawdown limit

Q
Q&A Preparation — Know These Cold

  • Why this specific strategy vs. the two alternatives?
  • What is the total cost of the hedge as % of AUM?
  • What happens to the hedge if VIX spikes to 40?
  • What is the delta of your put position?
  • What happens after expiration — do you renew?
  • How does the premium cost affect the Sharpe Ratio?

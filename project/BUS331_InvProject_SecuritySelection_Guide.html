The Security Selection Funnel

Phase 4 is a bottom-up process that must be anchored to your top-down Phase 1 macro thesis. Every security you choose must pass through three gates before it earns a place in the portfolio. If a security cannot survive the Macro Filter, it does not belong in the portfolio — regardless of how attractive it looks in isolation.

Universe of Securities
All publicly traded equities, ETFs, bonds, REITs, commodities, and derivatives
▼
Gate 1 — Macro Filter (Phase 1)
Does this asset class thrive in your forecasted economic regime?
▼
Gate 2 — IPS Suitability (Phase 2)
Does this security fit the client's risk, return, time horizon, tax, and liquidity needs?
▼
Gate 3 — CME Weight (Phase 3)
Does this security fit within the asset class weight Solver already optimized?
The 10-Security Minimum: Each client portfolio requires at least 10 securities spanning at least 3 distinct asset classes. No single security may exceed 25% of the portfolio. All weights must sum to exactly 100%.

Mandatory Derivative Hedging Strategy

Every client portfolio must include a derivative hedging strategy to floor potential losses in the Bear Case. You must evaluate all three strategies, document the comparison, and select and implement the one that best fits your client's IPS constraints. Your choice is graded — a strategy chosen without comparison or rationale earns no credit.

The Three Strategies You Must Evaluate: Every team must complete a comparison table covering all three options — even the ones you don't use. A strategy is "rejected" with documented reasoning, not simply ignored.
🛡️
Strategy A — Protective Put
Pay a premium · Floor losses · Keep full upside
MechanismBuy put options on an underlying you own
CostOption premium (paid upfront)
Downside ProtectionComplete below strike price
Upside ImpactUnlimited upside retained (net of premium)
Best forRisk-averse clients with hard drawdown limits
Key RiskPremium is lost if market rallies (insurance cost)
📞
Strategy B — Covered Call
Collect premium · Cap upside · Partial downside offset
MechanismSell call options on a position you hold
CostZero cost — you receive premium income
Downside ProtectionOnly to the extent of premium received — no floor
Upside ImpactCapped at strike price — you give away gains above strike
Best forNeutral-to-modest-upside outlook; income generation
Key RiskDoes NOT protect against a large drawdown — misleading as a "hedge"
📊
Strategy C — Futures Contract
Short the market · Zero-cost · Full hedge ratio control
MechanismShort index futures (e.g., E-mini S&P 500) to offset portfolio losses
CostMargin deposit (not a sunk cost — returned at close)
Downside ProtectionGains on short futures offset equity losses dollar-for-dollar
Upside ImpactShort position loses if market rallies — directly reduces upside
Best forTactical, temporary hedging with precision control of hedge ratio
Key RiskRequires active management; margin calls if market moves against you
📊 Strategy C Deep Dive — Futures Contract Mechanics

A futures hedge uses a short position in an index futures contract to offset the market risk of your equity portfolio. Unlike options, futures have no upfront premium — but they require a margin deposit and must be actively managed. The key calculation is the hedge ratio: how many contracts do you need to short to fully offset your portfolio's market exposure?

Hedge Ratio = (Portfolio Value × Portfolio Beta) ÷ (Futures Price × Contract Multiplier)
// Number of futures contracts to short for a full hedge

E-mini S&P 500 (ES): Multiplier = $50 per index point
Micro E-mini (MES): Multiplier = $5 per index point   // Better for smaller portfolios

Margin vs. Premium — Key Distinction: A futures margin deposit is not a cost — it is collateral returned when the position is closed. This differs fundamentally from an option premium, which is a sunk cost paid upfront and non-refundable. When comparing strategies in your Comparison Table, do not treat margin as equivalent to option cost.
Roll Strategy: Futures contracts expire quarterly (Mar, Jun, Sep, Dec). If your hedge needs to remain in place beyond the expiration date, you must "roll" the contract — close the expiring position and open the next contract. Document your roll strategy in the Derivatives Tab of your Excel workbook.

Payoff Diagram Comparison — All Three Strategies at Expiration

Underlying = SPY at $520. Strike = $510. Premium = $8/share. Futures short entry at $520.

Reading the diagram: Protective Put floors losses below the strike — the position does not keep losing as the market falls. Covered Call caps gains above the strike — you forfeit upside to collect the premium. Short Futures is a symmetric hedge — gains when the market falls, but loses symmetrically when the market rises.
